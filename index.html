Fractals

Fractal (Latin fractus – broken, partial, fractional) in colloquial terms usually denotes a self-similar object, meaning one whose parts are similar to the whole, or "infinitely complex" revealing increasingly intricate details at any arbitrarily large magnification. Due to the immense variety of examples, mathematicians currently refrain from providing a strict definition and spropose to characterize a fractal as a set that possesses all the characteristics below or at least the majority of them:

  • has a non-trivial structure at every scale
  • its structure is not easily describable in the language of traditional Euclidean geometry
  • is self-similar, if not in an exact sense, then in an approximate or stochastic sense
  • its Hausdorff dimension is greater than its topological dimension
  • has a relatively simple recursive definition
  • has a natural appearance (irregular, intricate, etc.)
